NYC Link Liberation Front
BACK TO MAP

LinkNYC kiosks were presented as 21st-century utilitarian stations that would grease the wheels of city life as phone booths once did. They offered free phone calls within the U.S., wif-fi, device charging, and directions.

What arrived on our streets were monoliths that function as digital advertising billboards, data collection apparati, and potential surveillance systems.

About 1,800 Links of the 7,500 promised Links have been installed in NYC. Most of them are located in Manhattan where wi-fi access is prevalent, but selling ads is easier. Many of the city's poorest areas have few kiosks or none at all.

The makers of the Links, in their techno-capitalist myopia, overlooked who would become the primary user of the Link: our neighbors who are without homes.

Many of these neighbors lack smartphones, computers, and access to the Internet and entertainment. Some built encampments at the Links, their light and structure acting as a kind of techno-hearth. For those of us without a home, what is private is made public. And so, with the initially unrestricted Internet access, many users utilized the Links for entertainment.

This led to an outcry as the unintended users of Link towers and their actions were visually offensive to the middle class. Web-browsing was suspended. The public sphere was secured again for the sake of advertising and consumerism, this time with a technologically advanced apparatus, one that could easily be used to surveil the public 24/7.

Let us dream of a world without advertising and surveillance of the sort that litters our public spaces under contemporary capitalism. What should the Links be used for and whom should they serve?
